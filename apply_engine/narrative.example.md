I'm an engineer who got here by being relentlessly curious about how things actually work, and by
refusing to stop at the surface. I trained in mechanical/simulation engineering, but the part I
loved was never one tool — it was connecting across domains: physics, optimization, software, and
the people problem of getting a team to ship something hard. I chose breadth on purpose. I've gone
deep enough to be a specialist in several areas, but my real edge is moving between them.

The way I work is a loop: learn something deeply, build the workflow that turns that understanding
into output, then automate it so it runs without me. I build the thing I need before anyone assigns
it. In the last two years that loop went AI-native. I architect and operate systems that LLM coding
harnesses implement under my direction — at work I shipped five automation tools that took manual
analysis from days to minutes, and on my own time I built ARIA, a personal multi-agent system that
runs my career, finances, and research. I own the whole loop: design, agent and prompt work,
debugging, and day-to-day operation.

What drives me is wanting to win at the highest level with people I respect. I'm a lifelong
competitor; engineering is how I compete now. I'm drawn to teams building AI-native systems because
that's the natural extension of how I already work, and because the leverage is enormous: a
generalist who can direct AI to ship real systems is the highest-output profile I know how to be.
