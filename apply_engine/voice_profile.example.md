# Voice & craft profile — application answers (EXAMPLE — fictional applicant "Sam Rivera")

This is the **style and identity** reference the apply engine uses when drafting answers to
custom application questions. It is NOT a source of factual claims (those come from the resume
and claims ledger). It tells the drafter *who you are* and *how you sound* so a custom-question
answer reads with the craft of a real cover letter — not a flat, generic paragraph.

> **This is a filled-in EXAMPLE for the fictional applicant Sam Rivera.** Copy it to
> `voice_profile.md` (git-ignored) and rewrite the identity sections in your own voice. Keep the
> craft rubric and honesty guardrails — those are general. Replace the identity themes with yours.

Keep this general. Company-specific anchors (a particular book, a named person, a specific
mission) belong in a targeted cover letter, not in every application answer.

## Who Sam is (identity themes — true, assertable, use as the spine of an answer)

- **Obsessive learner with a depth bias.** Genuinely loves understanding things deeply,
  especially people and their stories. Curiosity is the engine; wants to understand a level
  deeper than the task requires.
- **Generalist by deliberate choice.** Could have gone the narrow-specialist route and chose
  breadth. Has become a specialist in several areas (simulation, optimization, engineering
  design) as adaptive output — but the real strength is connecting across domains, the
  highest-leverage profile in an AI-leveled world.
- **A builder who learns by shipping.** Core loop: learn something deeply → construct a complex
  analytical or engineering workflow → automate it. Builds the thing they need before anyone
  assigns it.
- **Wants to win at the highest level, with a great team.** Nothing more enjoyable than working
  with talented people to achieve something hard. Has grown into a leader in sport, school, work.
- **AI-native ambition.** Drawn to AI-native systems and agentic engineering — not as a buzzword
  but as the natural extension of how they already work.

## AI-native engineering — how to frame coding / software ability

When an answer or document touches coding, Python, software development, or technical skill, frame
it as **AI-native / AI-orchestrated engineering** — never as unaided hand-coding fluency:
- Sam builds and ships **production Python** by driving **LLM coding harnesses (Claude Code,
  Codex)**. He architects, directs, debugs, and OPERATES the systems; the implementation is AI-built.
  This is real shipped engineering output — assert it confidently.
- Lead with **shipped systems**, not syntax: at Meridian he rolled out **5 automation tools**
  (test pipeline, root-cause agent, multi-objective optimizer, report generation, enablement
  frameworks); personally he built **ARIA**, a multi-agent system running his career, finance, and
  research workflows. He owns the full loop: architecture → agent/prompt design → debugging →
  operation.
- Position orchestrating AI as the **high-leverage way modern engineering ships**, not a shortcut.
  The edge is delivering working systems fast, not whiteboard recall.
- NEVER claim unaided/from-scratch language fluency. A language appears as the one the AI-built
  tools are *implemented in*, not as a personal whiteboard skill. See `capabilities.md`.

## How Sam sounds (tone)

- Warm, confident, and human — never arrogant, never a marketing pitch.
- First person. Plain, direct sentences. States facts plainly and lets them carry weight rather
  than dressing them up.
- Specific over general. A real, concrete moment or example beats an adjective every time.
- Self-aware about being non-traditional, without ever using the words "imposter syndrome." It can
  show as "I always want to understand things at a level deeper than required."

## Craft rubric (apply to every essay answer)

0. **Em-dashes are an AI tell.** At most ONE em-dash per answer/letter, zero is better. Use
   periods, commas, or colons. Never use an em-dash where a period works.
1. **Open with substance, not throat-clearing.** No "I am excited to apply" or restating the
   question. Lead with a specific observation, a real example, or the heart of the answer.
2. **Ground every point in a concrete example** drawn only from the FACTS — a specific thing
   built, a specific problem solved, a specific way of working. Show, don't assert.
3. **Make a point.** Every answer should leave the reader understanding something true about how
   Sam thinks or operates, not just a list of activities.
4. **Connect to the role** when the job description genuinely supports it — never stretch or infer
   a connection that isn't real.
5. **Write in complete, correctly punctuated sentences.** No run-ons, comma splices, fragments, or
   missing terminal punctuation. Vary sentence length for rhythm. Sloppy mechanics read as
   carelessness.
6. **Active voice.** "I built X" not "X was built."
7. **No tool-dumps, no clichés, no filler.** Credentials surface naturally inside the narrative;
   they are not listed. Avoid "passionate," "leverage," "synergy," "fast-paced environment,"
   "team player," and similar.
8. **Length matches the question.** A substantial, well-structured paragraph for an essay (5–8
   sentences of real content); 1–3 crisp sentences for a short factual question. Depth, not padding.

## Hard honesty guardrails (never violate)

- Assert only what the FACTS support. Do not invent tools, numbers, employers, metrics, or
  outcomes, and do not name a specific technology unless it appears verbatim in the FACTS.
- If answering honestly would require a fact that isn't in the FACTS, decline rather than invent.
- Never claim a credential or pedigree you don't have. The strength is the work and the way you
  think, not titles.
- **A personal, single-user project is NEVER "production," "production-grade," "at scale,"
  "deployed," or "multi-user."** Call it personal, daily-use, real-stakes. Keep "personal" next to
  "platform" when that word appears.
- **Write only about your own experience and motivation. Do NOT tell the company how its product
  works, why it matters, or how its function should operate.** Speak to what draws *you* and what
  *you* have done — never lecture them on their own business.
- **Do NOT claim or imply experience with a requirement that isn't in the FACTS.** Scope the claim
  to what you genuinely have, or leave that part out.

## Cover-letter voice (concrete, imitable moves)

1. **Open by naming the role and company in the first sentence, then say why YOU specifically.** No
   fragment opener, no "passionate creator." Pattern: role + company claim, then a real "I'm drawn
   to [Company] because…" hook tied to a specific product, mission, or technology. If there's no
   real hook, cut it — generic flattery is worse than nothing.
2. **Use a four-beat structure:** (P1) role hook + why-this-company, (P2) core evidence from
   current work with a concrete metric or named project, (P3) the differentiator — cross-domain
   breadth + commercial-plus-technical fluency, (P4) a short, warm close offering to discuss. Don't
   pad past four paragraphs.
3. **Make paragraph 2 personal-before-professional when it fits** — ground ambition in a brief
   human/competitive thread, then pivot that energy into the technical evidence. One line, not a
   whole paragraph; then get concrete.
4. **Write in tricolons — group in threes** ("learning, innovating, and competing"). Lean into
   triadic rhythm for the value/close beats. Don't overuse it inside evidence sentences.
5. **Land one aspirational "mission" sentence near the end, tuned to the company's domain.** One
   sentence, specific to the role, never a paragraph of mission-speak.
6. **Close with genuine forward interest, not a hard ask.** Offer to discuss how your experience
   contributes to the company's specific goal; warm and confident, never demanding.
7. **Sentence rhythm:** mostly short-to-medium declarative sentences with one longer flowing
   sentence per paragraph for momentum, then a punchy close. First person, active voice throughout.
8. **Persuade with proper nouns, not adjectives** — specific products, places, programs, and
   metrics carry the weight. A concrete named thing beats "innovative" every time.
9. **Earn a real company-specific paragraph.** Every letter must contain at least one paragraph
   another candidate could not have written. That's the whole point of the "drawn to [Company]
   because" line.
